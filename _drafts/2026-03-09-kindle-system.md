## How I Organized 800 Kindle Books Using the 10-3-1 Rule

Over the weekend I ran into a small but frustrating problem.

I wanted to read a specific book on my Kindle, and I couldn’t find it.

Not because it wasn’t there. I have nearly **800 books** on my Kindle. Somewhere inside that digital pile was the book I wanted. I just couldn’t remember the title.

So I started scrolling.

Then searching.

Then scrolling again.

I spent far too long trying to remember the name of a book I knew I owned.

Eventually I found it, but the whole experience revealed something obvious: my Kindle had quietly turned into a **disorganized digital bookstore**.

Part of the reason is how I buy books.

When I see a book that looks interesting, I don’t ask myself if I’ll read it this week or this month. I simply ask one question:

**Do I want to read this sometime in my life?**

If the answer is yes, I buy it.

Over the years that simple rule quietly built a library of hundreds of books. It’s a wonderful problem to have, but it also means the library can easily become overwhelming.

If you have hundreds of books, you probably know the feeling. When everything is available all the time, it becomes strangely difficult to choose what to read next.

A couple of weeks earlier I had organized my **physical bookshelves** at home. That felt satisfying, but honestly it wasn’t nearly as important as organizing my Kindle. My digital library was where most of the chaos lived.

So I decided to build a simple system.

Instead of organizing books by subject like a bookstore—philosophy, psychology, business, and so on—I organized them by **reading decisions**.

Here is the system I ended up with:

1. Reading Now
2. Next
3. Reference / Re-Read
4. Influential
5. Abandoned
6. Someday

“Reading Now” contains the small handful of books I’m actively reading.

“Next” is a short queue of what I plan to read after I finish the current ones.

“Reference / Re-Read” holds books worth returning to later.

“Influential” is reserved for the books that actually shape how I think. These are the rare books that change your perspective or stay with you long after you close them.

Then there’s “Abandoned.”

For years I felt a quiet guilt about not finishing books. But the truth is that many books contain one good idea stretched across hundreds of pages. Once you understand the idea, forcing yourself to finish the book doesn’t necessarily add much.

So now when a book stops holding my attention, I move it into **Abandoned**. The decision is made, and I move on.

No guilt.

Finally there is **Someday**, which holds most of the other books in my library. It’s the place where interesting books wait until I decide to promote them into the “Next” collection.

This system works especially well because of a simple reading principle I follow called the **10-3-1 Rule**.

Out of every ten books I start:

- **Three** are worth reading seriously.
    
- **One** becomes truly influential.
    

The other six are simply part of the search.

That’s normal. It’s how thoughtful reading works.

The surprising result of this system is **calm**.

When I open my Kindle now, I can instantly answer three simple questions:

What am I reading now?  
What will I read next?  
Which books have actually shaped my thinking?

Everything else is just storage.

In a world overflowing with information, sometimes the most fulfilling thing we can do is create a little order in the places that matter.

A small system like this doesn’t just organize books.

It makes reading thoughtful again.

And thoughtful reading is one of the quiet ways we build a meaningful life.
